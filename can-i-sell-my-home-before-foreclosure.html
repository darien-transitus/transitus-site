The fear it's too late to sell before the auction

Written by Transitus · Denver, Colorado · June 21, 2026

You keep circling the same question, and I want to answer it before anything else. Can you sell your home before the foreclosure auction? Yes. You can. The auction feels like a wall coming straight at you, like a date someone else circled on a calendar that you can't get in front of. I see why it feels that way. But it isn't a wall, and you are not as out of moves as that quiet fear is telling you. The home is still yours right now, and so is the choice. Let me walk you through how this actually works, plainly, the way I would if we were sitting across the table from each other.

Yes, and you don't need the bank's blessing to do it

Here is the part I wish someone had said to you on day one. For a regular sale, the kind where the home is worth more than you owe, you do not need your servicer's permission to sell. You can put it on the market and sell it like any other owner would. The buyer's money pays off the loan, and the bank doesn't get to stand in the doorway and stop you. That surprises a lot of people, because everything else in this process makes it feel like the bank is holding all the cards. On this one, you hold them.

Selling is one of several honest ways out of foreclosure, not a failure and not something to be ashamed of. Foreclosure itself ends in a public auction where the home goes to the highest bidder, and getting ahead of that, on your own terms, is almost always the better position to be in. So if part of you felt like asking the question at all was admitting defeat, please let that go. Asking is you taking the wheel back.

More time than the fear is telling you

Before we go further, breathe. The auction is almost certainly not tomorrow. In most cases the formal foreclosure can't even begin until you are more than 120 days behind on the loan (12 CFR 1024.41(f)(1)), so there is usually a stretch of months to list and sell before anything official starts at all.

And once it does start, you still have room. In a Colorado public trustee foreclosure of a normal residential property, the sale has to be set no sooner than 110 and no later than 125 days after the bank records its notice (CRS 38-38-108(1)(a)). That is roughly three and a half to four months from the official start to the auction. I am not telling you this to make you feel a clock ticking. I am telling you because that time is real, and it is yours to use. The hard truth that comes with it is simple: that window is finite, and the days run whether you act or not. So sooner is calmer, and sooner gives you more choices than later ever will.

The quiet relief of getting to keep what's yours

If your home is worth more than what you owe, this is the path I most want you to see clearly, because there is genuine good news folded inside it. When you sell, the proceeds pay off the mortgage in full, you cover the costs of selling, and whatever is left over is yours to keep. Not the bank's. Yours. That is money you would very likely lose if the home went to auction instead.

In Colorado, what actually clears the loan off your home is the debt being paid in full and the lender asking the public trustee to release it (CRS 38-39-102(1)(a)). There is no secret approval step where the bank gets to hold your sale hostage. So if you have equity, you have leverage, and you have a way out that protects both your money and your credit. I know it has felt like every option was being taken from you. This one was here the whole time.

Harder if you're underwater, but still not the end

Now the harder truth, and I won't soften it, because you deserve it straight. If you owe more than the home is worth, you can't just sell it on your own terms. Selling for less than the balance is called a short sale, and it can only happen if your lender agrees to it. I have to be honest with you here: the bank is never required to say yes. Federal rules say that if you turn in a complete application more than 37 days before the sale, the servicer has to look at your options and give you a written answer within 30 days (12 CFR 1024.41(c)(1)). What those rules do not do is force the bank to approve anything (12 CFR 1024.41(a)). They have to look. They don't have to agree, and I know that can be a hard thing to sit with.

There is one trap I really don't want you to fall into. Even after a short sale goes through, the lender can sometimes still come after you for the difference between what the home sold for and what you owed. Because of that, many people in your shoes ask the lender to waive that difference and get the waiver in writing. That single piece of paper, when it's granted, can be what separates walking away clean from a debt that trails you for years. If that feels like one more thing to track in an already exhausting process, I understand. It matters, so please don't let it slip by.

When the clock and the closing are racing each other

You might be thinking, that's all fine, but how do I sell before the auction when finding a buyer and closing takes weeks? This is the place where the law quietly works in your favor. A short sale counts as loss mitigation, so when you turn in a complete application on time, the servicer can't push the foreclosure forward or hold the sale while it reviews you (12 CFR 1024.41(g)).

Colorado backs that up with its own protection. If you have the servicer's written confirmation of a complete application dated at least 37 days before the sale, and you get it to the foreclosure officer no later than 14 calendar days before the sale date, the officer has to pause the sale while they confirm it with your servicer (CRS 38-38-103.2(3)). On top of that, a Colorado sale can be postponed for good cause, or at the lender's written request, for as long as twelve months from the original date (CRS 38-38-109(1)(a)). None of this happens on its own, and the deadlines are firm, so this is the part where you move with urgency. But it means the act of pursuing a sale can buy you the very weeks you need to finish one. I know you may not have wanted any of this, and if it comes down to selling the home you've lived in, that is draining in a way few people understand. I see that. I do. But to bring it back to the question you walked in with: yes, you can sell before the auction. If you have equity, a regular sale is yours to make without the bank's permission. If you're underwater, a short sale is still possible. And the law gives you ways to buy the time a sale needs to close.
